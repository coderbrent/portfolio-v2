About Me

Ah, the dreaded "About Me" page.

Like most people, I could probably sit and talk about myself for hours given the opportunity. On the other hand, I can't stand listening to someone else droll on about themselves, so allow me to hurry along with this section!

Professional Stuff

Outside of a few retail gigs in my late teens and early twenties, I've spent the majority of my working life as a business owner in the Limousine industry. Back in 2012, much to my wife's dismay, I took the last $5k I had in the bank and started a car service I called Black Rock Limousine. Things got off to a great start and I grew sales from $0 (of course) to just under $400k in 3 years of operation.

As the business grew, I began driving less and hiring more. Things began rapidly taking off. So much so, that by mid-2015, I found myself acquiring a local competitor company, Red Bank Limo. With the acquisition, our fleet grew to 10 vehicles and our employee count to 22. By the end of 2016, the merged company was hovering just around the $1 million mark in annual revenue. We were transporting celebrities, athletes, CEO's and hundreds of other local travelers.

So why on Earth did I decide to give up on a successful business to become a developer of all things?

Simply put - I love computers. I always have. My first computer was a broken Tandy 2000 that my Grandmother brought home from her office for me to play with in the late 80's. In middle school I was exposed to computer programming via LOGO - an educational programming language designed to teach kids graphical programming (does anyone else remember the turtle?). Years later, I would spend hours tinkering with Visual Basic 3.0 (and eventually, 4.0!) on our family speed demon 233mhz Packard Bell desktop PC. Steve Case never knew what him him.

It only took one in depth conversation with my wife to point me towards my passion. When my daughter was born in 2016, I knew I needed security. With current advancements in technology, I feared (along with many of my colleagues) that my industry was living on borrowed time... to be cont.
